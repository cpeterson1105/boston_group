Electric Vehicles Help Reduce Carbon Dioxide Emissions

As part of their internal combustion process, traditional gas-powered cars produce an estimated 404 grams of carbon dioxide emissions per mile. These emissions primarily come in the form of tailpipe exhaust, which negatively impacts air quality in addition to contributing to climate change. Because electric vehicles do not use internal combustion, they produce no tailpipe exhaust. This ensures electric vehicles' net carbon dioxide emissions are much smaller.

Carbon dioxide levels are 43 percent higher than they were prior to the Industrial Revolution. Research has shown that higher levels of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases trap heat in Earth's atmosphere that would otherwise escape to space. This has resulted in a global temperature increase of 2 degrees Fahrenheit--a change large enough to melt polar ice caps, degrade air quality, and result in extreme weather patterns. Although personal vehicles are not solely responsible for this shift, they accocunt for roughly one-fifth of greenhouse gas emissions in the United States.

Electric Vehicles Are Poised To Become More Popular In Boston

Assuming that local growth in electric vehicles mirrors global trends, Boston's electric vehicle stock will increase to 59,000 cars by the end of 2030.

The "Vision and Action Plan GO BOSTON 2030" shows that there are only 351 electric vehicles in Boston, 2014. However, according to the Global EV Outlook 2019, the number of EVs stock is going to constantly increase. In case of the EV30@30 Scenario that set the objective to reach a 30% sales share for EVs by 2030, the numbe will reach almost 200 times of the stock of 2015. If Boston succeed to follow the expansion, the EVs in the city will exceed 59 thousand. Furthermore, given the Boston is a metropolitan city where has faster growth potential than the average of world. In fact, an estimation by the International Council on Clean Transportation shows more ambitious predictions.
